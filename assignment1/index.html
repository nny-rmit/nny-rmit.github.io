Introduction

For this assignment, I chose to analyse the website which hosts Theo Ellin Ballew's essay "A New Legibility", noting down it's unique interactions and visual language.


You can find it here.

Q1. What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

Upon first opening the site, The only things that call to attention are the starting bubble, and the intearctive trail that follows the cursor. The design langauge used here is minimalist but sophisticated, communicated by the use of a serif typeface.

Q2. Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

Here are the discrete actions I noticed; responsive trail following behind the cursor, ability to move text boxes and the ability to close them, ability to move the disclaimer bar at the bottom which covers up the front page, changing the settings to reveal all possible textboxes or making them smaller, clickable links that cause new text boxes to appear, clickable links that lead off-site to other pages, and clicking a link that leads to an already-present box causes it to flash yellow.

Q3. What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

Due to the minimalist and simplistic nature of the site's interactions, most time was spent combing through text to find new links to click on, and continuing the train of thought, only to move onto another thread of links going in another thematic direction.

Q4. What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

The most common action was either the arranging of textboxes to make space for another to be manageable, or clicking on links.

Q5. What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

The primary objective of the site is explicitly to host an essay, but the way in which this function is explored adds a game-like element to it that allows for several different topics to be explored alongside one another, in a way that traditional paragraph-based layout do not allow for, goading the user to continue on and trawl through.

Q6. How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

This exploration aspect allows for an inquisitive approach to reading the content and deciding what logical thread to pursue next, with the goal of looking for the final textbox (in which the "prize" is learning the names of the boxes - a "gobbet"), motivating the user forward to 'play the game' à la clicking more links.

Q7. What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time? (For how long and how many different times)

Despite the non-linear approach to displaying the site's content, the impression is that this site requires only 1 visit, 2 at the most, as the site controls make it easy to dismiss textboxes and move them into a comfortable configuration to make reading easier. Thus, reading the whole essay all the way through would seem to take ~25-30 minutes in total, thanks to the modular UI, though this number will differ based on the person.

Q8. How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

The experience doesn't explicitly communicate any particular intended way of interacting with it over a select period, instead allowing the user to approach the barebones UI that exists and manipulate it in their own way.

Q9. What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

This experience makes references to interactive non-linear media such as choose-your-own-adventure novels, volvelles, and literary experiments such as Cortazar's 'Hopscotch' and Nabokov's 'Pale Fire'.

Q10. What do these references communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

The literary contemporaries the experience references explore a common theme and goal, to give readers more agency when approaching text, to allow them to explore the narrative in a non-linear fashion. In a similar way, the 'hypertextual' interface allows for that modularity and agency, communicating that you, the user, are free to engage the features of the site to its limits and pick apart anything that triggers intrigue.

Q11. What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

What these references imply is that one should have an inquisitive and curious attitude to navigating the site; to explore and engage with what insights the text has to offer.

Q12. What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

The most frustrating aspect of the experience's design is also its greatest strength, both narratively and functionally. The clutter as each box is opened and moved, only to link back to a previous box creates a convoluting loop of retreading your steps to find a particular topic or point. And yet, it's also precisely this design choice that echoes the author's ethos of sezing the attention of a modern audience and how internet-based text has developed. In a way, it mimics the stream-of-thought consciousness technique for writing, putting the user in the shoes of the author's unfiltered first thoughts, which makes the frustration feel intentional.

Q13. What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

Reaching the end of the work was the most satisfying aspect of this experience, with the satisfaction itself being less of a result of the actual manouevring required to reach that point via the arranging of textboxes, and moreso a combination of overcoming the interface and reaching the culmination of the essay. Thus the satisfaction is derived from both the mechanical and narrative components of the entire experience, fitting for one as brief and minimal as this essay.

The signifier of the final text box's glow being a different colour, and the tidbit about the author's name for the main elements is a cheeky payoff that feels rewarding, even menially.
